\begin{abstract}
We set out to train a tabula-rasa Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)
agent to drive 200--800\,m urban routes in CARLA~0.9.15 from a single
semantic-segmentation camera plus a 10-dimensional state vector,
using only a sparse progress-and-terminal reward (no expert
demonstrations, no hand-engineered behaviors). Over roughly
72~hours of iteration we executed six training runs---three pure-RL,
three Behavior Cloning (BC) warmstart~+~PPO finetune---each
pre-registered against explicit falsification criteria drawn from
the RL literature. All six runs plateaued within a narrow
4.4\%--5.9\% rolling route-completion (RC) band despite targeted
hyperparameter interventions spanning entropy scheduling, log-std
clamping, Ng-Harada-Russell potential shaping, KL-clipping, and
learning-rate reduction. A standalone diagnostic script, written
\emph{after} the sixth failure and \emph{independent} of the training
loop, revealed that an early infrastructure commit---which added
\texttt{tm.set\_hybrid\_physics\_mode(True)} to the CARLA
TrafficManager as a defensive ``NPCs not moving'' fix---was itself
freezing roughly 70\% of NPC vehicles at spawn across every run and
every BC data collection. The final deliverable is therefore a
\emph{frozen} BC policy, trained on 100\,k expert frames and
evaluated deterministically on 20~episodes after the bug was removed:
\textbf{7.42\% mean RC, 32.93\% max}, 0\% route-complete,
75\% collision. The 75\% collision rate is a textbook
Codevilla-2019 distribution-shift signature---BC was trained against
frozen NPCs and evaluated against moving ones. The scientific
contribution of this project is therefore not a trained driving
policy but a reproducible, pre-registered catalogue of six
reward-design and imitation-learning failure modes in CARLA, each
tied to a specific theorem or published empirical result, and the
demonstration that a single environmental bug can silently
contaminate an entire training pipeline in ways that neither the
reward curve nor standard PPO diagnostics will reveal.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Urban autonomous driving from a single camera is an archetypal
high-dimensional continuous-control problem: the action space is
low-dimensional (throttle/brake and steering), but the observation
space is large (image pixels), the reward is sparse (most steps are
``keep going''), and the failure modes are many (collisions,
off-route, deadlock, stagnation). CARLA~0.9.15~\cite{carla} provides
a photorealistic simulator for this problem, and recent work has
demonstrated that PPO~\cite{ppo} with a deliberately minimal reward
of the form ``progress along planned route plus terminal reward''
can reach $\sim$85\% Leaderboard score at $100\text{M}$~steps
$\times$ 16~environments (CaRL, Jaeger~et~al.\ 2025~\cite{carl}).
The same line of work and its imitation-learning
predecessors---Roach~\cite{roach}, LBC~\cite{lbc}, LAV~\cite{lav}---
collectively establish the template for a solo course project:
start with the minimal reward, train PPO, report the numbers.

This report documents the opposite outcome. Across six documented
training runs totalling approximately 15~hours of wall-clock compute,
the UrbanZero agent never exceeded 5.9\% rolling route-completion in
either pure-RL or BC-warmstart-plus-finetune configurations. Every
published intervention we attempted---entropy-coefficient
annealing~\cite{andrychowicz}, log-std upper-clamping into
Andrychowicz~et~al.'s viable band~\cite{andrychowicz}, potential-based
shaping in the Ng-Harada-Russell sense~\cite{ng}, KL-to-BC
regularization from Roach~\cite{roach}, widened action-noise during
BC data collection \`a~la DAgger~\cite{dagger}---was necessary but
not sufficient. What finally explained the consistent plateau was a
single line of CARLA TrafficManager configuration code that had been
added weeks earlier as a defensive fix and had been silently freezing
the majority of dynamic NPC traffic on every run.

\textbf{The contributions of this paper are:}

\textbf{C1 --- A complete, reproducible CARLA PPO~+~BC pipeline}
covering environment wrapper, Gym reward, custom CNN + state-MLP
feature extractor, clamped-Gaussian policy, BC data collection from
\texttt{BehaviorAgent}, Gaussian-NLL BC trainer with frame-stack
support, ROS~2 integration, launch tooling, and beacon telemetry.
Every component is on GitHub with the commit history that produced it.

\textbf{C2 --- A pre-registered failure catalogue} of six training
runs. For each run we state, \emph{before} launch, the falsification
criteria drawn from published RL pathology literature, the expected
behavior if the hypothesis holds, and the exact beacon metrics that
would refute it. Each of the six runs falsified its own hypothesis,
and the failure mode is named and cited.

\textbf{C3 --- A root-cause discovery via standalone diagnostic.}
After the sixth failure, rather than mutating the training loop
again, we wrote a 150-line script that spawned NPCs with the
existing \texttt{\_spawn\_traffic()} code path and measured their
position every tick. The script isolated
\texttt{tm.set\_hybrid\_physics\_mode(True)} as the source of the
NPC-freeze in under ten minutes---an observation that no PPO metric
would have produced, because at the policy level the frozen-NPC
world simply looks like an unusually deterministic environment with
poor generalization.

We believe C3 is the most transferable lesson of this project. The
infrastructure bug was introduced with a paper-cited rationale
(CARLA issue \#3860), reviewed by multiple agents, and executed
faithfully for five prior runs. It was only caught by leaving the
training loop and asking the environment a direct question in
isolation. Section~\ref{sec:lessons} expands on this and five
related lessons.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{CARLA PPO.} CaRL~\cite{carl} is the direct ancestor of the
reward function used here: \emph{reward~$=$~$0.05 \cdot
\min(\Delta\text{progress}, \text{cap}) + 0.005 \cdot
\text{speed\_carrot} + \text{terminal}~(\pm 50)$}. CaRL reaches
85\% Leaderboard at $100$M~steps~$\times$~16--300~envs; our compute
budget is approximately~$1/50$ of that.

\textbf{Behavior Cloning for CARLA.} Roach~\cite{roach} demonstrates
BC~+~PPO finetune with KL-to-BC regularization
($\beta = 0.1 \to 0.05$ over 2M~steps) reaching $\sim$80\%
Leaderboard. Learning by Cheating (LBC)~\cite{lbc} uses privileged
BEV-teacher to camera-student DAgger distillation~\cite{dagger}.
Codevilla~et~al.~\cite{codevilla} explicitly document the failure
mode our final eval exhibits: BC with MSE loss on multimodal expert
actions regresses to the mean of the action distribution, which
looks like correct driving until the distribution shifts at
deployment.

\textbf{Policy-gradient pathologies.} A legacy 7M-step run that
preceded this project converged to a perpendicular-circling policy
matching Krakovna~et~al.'s~\cite{krakovna} catalogue of
specification-gaming examples. Its root cause---a
$\texttt{speed\_reward} \cdot \cos(\text{angle})$ term that is
non-potential in the Ng-Harada-Russell sense~\cite{ng}---motivated
the minimal-reward rewrite analyzed here.

\textbf{Exploration pressure.} Andrychowicz~et~al.~\cite{andrychowicz}
benchmark \texttt{ent\_coef} $\in [0.003, 0.03]$ and
$\sigma \in [0.3, 0.7]$ as the viable band for continuous control
from scratch. Rajeswaran~et~al.~\cite{rajeswaran} show that annealing
entropy-coefficient to zero during imitation finetuning collapses
the policy back to random. Both results directly shape the
hyperparameter schedule used in every run of this paper.

\textbf{RL evaluation rigor.} Henderson~et~al.~\cite{henderson} show
that RL results drawn before $\sim$3M clean steps are unreliable.
Pardo~et~al.~\cite{pardo} give the correct GAE bootstrap under
episode truncation (e.g., our \texttt{REALLY\_STUCK} truncation).
Engstrom~et~al.~\cite{engstrom} show that PPO implementation
details (reward normalization, advantage normalization, etc.)
frequently dominate algorithmic choices.

\section{Technical Approach}

\subsection{Environment and observation}

UrbanZero wraps CARLA~0.9.15 in a Gym environment
(\texttt{env/carla\_env.py}) running synchronously at 20\,Hz
($\Delta t = 0.05$\,s) with \texttt{DummyVecEnv} serial
parallelization over two CARLA servers on ports 2000 and 3000. An
ego vehicle is spawned on Town01 and given a 200--800\,m planned
route via \texttt{GlobalRoutePlanner}. Each episode spawns 30~NPC
vehicles and 10~pedestrians controlled by the TrafficManager.

The observation is a Dict space. The \texttt{image} channel is
$(1,128,128)$ float32~$\in [0,1]$---single-channel normalized
semantic segmentation (class labels~$/~27$). The \texttt{state}
channel is $(10,)$ float32 in approximately~$[-1.5, 1.5]$,
encoding: speed $/$ \texttt{MAX\_SPEED}; three future waypoints in
ego-frame~$(dx, dy)$; signed lane offset $/$ 5\,m; traffic-light
state (0 none, 0.33 green, 0.67 yellow, 1.0 red/unknown); and
route-completion fraction. \texttt{VecFrameStack(n\_stack=4)}
supplies temporal context, yielding image $(4,128,128)$ and state
$(40,)$ after framestack.

Actions are continuous $\mathrm{Box}(-1, 1, \text{shape}=(2,))$.
Steering is $a_0$. The throttle-brake axis $a_1$ is shifted by
$+0.3$ before clipping, producing an idle-creep bias toward forward
motion~\cite{residual}:
\begin{align*}
  s       &= a_1 + 0.3 \\
  \text{throttle} &= \max(0, \min(1, s)) \\
  \text{brake}    &= \max(0, \min(1, -s)).
\end{align*}

\subsection{Reward function (CaRL-minimal profile)}

Five paper-cited terms, each with an explicit rationale:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Per-step reward term magnitudes (final profile).}
\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{@{}llr@{}}
\toprule
Term & Form & $|\cdot|_{\max}$ \\
\midrule
Progress          & $0.05 \cdot \min(\Delta_\text{route}, v_T\Delta t)$ & 0.021 \\
Velocity carrot   & $0.005 \cdot \min(v, v_T) / v_T$            & 0.005 \\
Idle cost         & $-0.15 \cdot \max(0, 1 - v)$                & 0.150 \\
Potential shaping & $\gamma\,\Phi(s') - \Phi(s)$                & 0.011 \\
Terminals         & $\pm 50$                                    & 50    \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\label{tbl:reward}
\end{table}

Progress and carrot are directly from CaRL~\cite{carl}. Idle cost
inverts the ``sit still is cheaper than crashing'' local optimum
observed in run~v1 (Sec.~\ref{sec:run1}). Potential shaping is
Ng-compliant~\cite{ng} and was added after v2 showed that progress
fires too sparsely to produce a steering gradient, with
$\Phi(s) = -0.015 \cdot \min(d_{2\mathrm{D}}(\text{ego},
\text{lookahead}), 30\text{\,m})$. The 240-to-1 shaping-to-terminal
ratio of the legacy 7M-step run was inverted to approximately
1-to-50, so that terminals dominate the undiscounted episode return.
See Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4} panel~(a) for per-term magnitudes.

\subsection{Policy}

\texttt{ClampedStdPolicy} (derived from SB3's
\texttt{ActorCriticPolicy}) soft-clamps
$\log\sigma \le \log(0.7) \approx -0.357$, targeting
Andrychowicz~et~al.'s~\cite{andrychowicz} upper viable-band edge. The
feature extractor \texttt{DrivingCNN} fuses a 5-layer Conv2d image
stream ($32 \to 64 \to 128 \to 128 \to 256$ channels with LayerNorm +
ReLU) with a 2-layer MLP on the 10-dim state
($\text{state} \to 64 \to 64$) and projects to a 256-dim shared
representation, feeding \texttt{net\_arch = dict(pi=[256,128],
vf=[256,128])}.

\subsection{BC pipeline}

\texttt{scripts/collect\_bc\_data.py} rolls out CARLA's
\texttt{BehaviorAgent("normal")} on Town01 with Gaussian noise
$\sigma = 0.1$\,rad injected into the steering channel
(DAgger-style~\cite{dagger}) and saves
(observation, action, episode\_start) tuples as \texttt{.npz}.
\texttt{agents/train\_bc.py} implements Gaussian-NLL training
against a model that exactly matches
\texttt{ClampedStdPolicy + DrivingCNN} so that the resulting
checkpoint loads into \texttt{PPO(policy=ClampedStdPolicy)} via
\texttt{PPO.load()} with a sibling \texttt{VecNormalize} pickle.

\subsection{Diagnostic methodology}

Every run wrote \texttt{\char`~/urbanzero/beacon.json} at 1\,Hz via
\texttt{eval/beacon\_callback.py}, reporting rolling windows of
\texttt{route\_completion}, \texttt{avg\_speed}, termination-reason
counts, \texttt{policy\_std}, \texttt{approx\_kl},
\texttt{clip\_fraction}, \texttt{entropy\_loss},
\texttt{explained\_variance}, and \texttt{ent\_coef}. These are the
metrics used in Sec.~\ref{sec:results}'s failure analysis. Each run
was also tagged with an \texttt{issue} number on the public GitHub
repo, where raw beacon JSON snapshots are preserved for replay.

\subsection{ROS~2 integration}

\texttt{ros/urbanzero\_node.py} runs as a ROS~2 Humble node
alongside training (launched in \texttt{run.sh} Pane~2). It
publishes four topics at 20\,Hz:
\texttt{/urbanzero/camera/semantic} (\texttt{sensor\_msgs/Image}
semantic-segmentation frames), \texttt{/urbanzero/vehicle/control}
(\texttt{geometry\_msgs/Twist} with $\text{linear.x} = a_1$ and
$\text{angular.z} = a_0$), \texttt{/urbanzero/vehicle/speed}
(\texttt{std\_msgs/Float32}), and \texttt{/urbanzero/status}
(\texttt{std\_msgs/String}). This satisfies the course's
ROS-and-sense-and-actuate requirement.

\subsection{Run 1 --- Sit-still attractor}
\label{sec:run1}

\textit{Hypothesis:} the CaRL-minimal reward plus the un-idled
legacy state is sufficient to produce forward motion.
\textit{Falsifier:} avg\_speed~$< 1$\,m/s at 100\,k steps.
\textit{Result:} falsified at 233\,k steps with
avg\_speed~$= 0.224$\,m/s and 70\% \texttt{REALLY\_STUCK}.
\textit{Analysis:} per-step cost of sitting
($-50/1500 = -0.033$) was cheaper than crashing
($-50/300 \approx -0.167$). Gradient correctly found the local
optimum; the reward permitted it~\cite{krakovna}.

\textit{Fix pushed:} \texttt{idle\_cost} $= -0.15 \cdot \max(0,
1 - v)$, a continuous ramp active only below 1\,m/s, making the
per-step cost of standing still $-0.183$---strictly worse than
crashing. The v1 hover-at-1\,m/s attractor documented in a legacy
run is avoided because the ramp has zero derivative above 1\,m/s.

\subsection{Run 2 --- \texorpdfstring{$\log \sigma$}{log-std} pinned at clamp}

\textit{Hypothesis:} with idle\_cost in place, pure-RL can find
forward motion and then steering. \textit{Falsifier:}
\texttt{policy\_std}~$\ge 0.95$ at 500\,k steps. \textit{Result:}
falsified at 892\,k steps with \texttt{policy\_std}~$= 0.999$
(pinned at the 1.0 upper clamp for $1500+$ consecutive episodes)
and \texttt{explained\_variance} oscillating from $-0.07$ to
$+0.91$. Rolling RC held at 5.4\%. \textit{Diagnosis:} at
$\sigma = 1.0$ the policy is essentially emitting pure noise; every
episode is a different random walk and the critic cannot
credit-assign to any systematic behavior~\cite{andrychowicz}.

\subsection{Run 3 --- Sparse steering gradient}

\textit{Hypothesis:} clamping $\log \sigma \le \log(0.7)$ and adding
Ng-compliant potential shaping gives a denser steering gradient.
\textit{Falsifier:} rolling RC $< 8\%$ at T+90\,min.
\textit{Result:} falsified at 104\,k steps / 15\,min.
\texttt{policy\_std}~$= 0.67$ (clamp held); termination distribution
split 50\%\,COLL / 41\%\,OFF\_ROUTE / 9\%\,REALLY\_STUCK.
\textbf{The PC-side operator reported during this run that spawned
NPCs were visibly frozen in both CARLA viewports} despite
\texttt{tm.set\_hybrid\_physics\_mode(True)} +
\texttt{tm.set\_synchronous\_mode(True)}. We missed this
observation---more precisely, we attributed it to ``hybrid physics
radius too small'' and pushed a commit halving \texttt{POTENTIAL\_K}
and adding \texttt{tm.global\_percentage\_speed\_difference(-30)}.
This was the first time the NPC bug was within arm's reach and was
not caught.

After three pure-RL failures the project pivoted to BC warmstart.

\subsection{Run 4 --- BC + PPO, KL runaway}

\textit{Hypothesis:} \texttt{bc\_pretrain.zip} (Gaussian-NLL-trained,
final NLL~$= -2.93$, MAE~$= 0.05$ on BehaviorAgent expert actions)
loaded into PPO with the pure-RL reward profile will produce
avg\_speed~$> 3$\,m/s from step~0 and rolling RC~$> 8\%$ within
5~minutes. \textit{Falsifier:} \texttt{approx\_kl}~$> 0.04$ at any
2048-step update. \textit{Result:} falsified at 28\,k steps.
\texttt{approx\_kl}~$= 0.079$ ($5\times$ the healthy target of
$0.015$), \texttt{clip\_fraction}~$= 0.34$,
\texttt{entropy\_loss}~$= +0.172$ (entropy \emph{decreasing},
$\sigma$ collapsing below BC's 0.22). Avg\_speed was high (7.6\,m/s)
for the first minute---BC prior clearly loaded---but RC collapsed
as PPO eroded the BC solution.

\subsection{Run 5 --- Same pathology, 3$\times$ slower}

\textit{Hypothesis:} reducing learning rate $3\mathrm{e}{-4} \to
1\mathrm{e}{-4}$ and \texttt{ent\_coef} $0.02 \to 0.005$ will slow
PPO's erosion of the BC prior enough for reward learning to
stabilize it. \textit{Falsifier:} \texttt{approx\_kl} $\ge$
v4\_kl after equivalent steps. \textit{Result:} falsified at 31\,k
with \texttt{approx\_kl}~$= 0.086$ (slightly \emph{worse} than~v4).
No measurable improvement from a 3$\times$ lr cut; the problem was
not update step size.

\subsection{Run 6 --- Widen-$\sigma$, $n_{ep}=1$, clip=0.1, 2.4M steps}

\textit{Hypothesis:} widening BC's $\log\sigma$ floor to
$\sigma = 0.5$, reducing PPO epochs to 1 per rollout, and halving
clip\_range to 0.1 will allow the reward to shape BC without
shredding it. \textit{Falsifier:} rolling RC~$< 8\%$ at T+30\,min.
\textit{Result:} soft-failed at 30\,min (RC~$= 4.67\%$,
avg\_speed~$1.7$\,m/s, 48\% \texttt{REALLY\_STUCK}); hard-failed at
5\,h / 2.4M steps (RC~$= 5.86\%$, avg\_speed~$1.0$\,m/s, 38\%
\texttt{REALLY\_STUCK}). PPO diagnostics were textbook-healthy
(\texttt{approx\_kl}~$= 0.0015$, \texttt{clip\_fraction}~$= 0.08$,
\texttt{explained\_variance}~$= 0.848$)---yet \emph{the actor was
frozen}. \texttt{entropy\_loss}~$= -1.74$ (entropy \emph{increasing},
$\sigma$ drifting upward $0.50 \to 0.58$ over 2.4M steps) despite a
decaying \texttt{ent\_coef}~$= 0.004$.

This was the point at which we stopped trusting PPO's internal
metrics to diagnose what was going on in the environment.

\subsection{Root cause: \texttt{tm.set\_hybrid\_physics\_mode(True)}}

Rather than launching a seventh run, we wrote
\texttt{scripts/sanity\_check\_npcs.py}---a 150-line standalone
script that invokes the \emph{same} \texttt{\_spawn\_traffic()} code
path used by training, then samples each NPC's
\texttt{get\_velocity()} for 100 ticks and reports the fraction that
moved more than 0.1\,m. Result on first invocation:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
NPCs spawned                             & 30 \\
Moving ($> 0.1$\,m in 100 ticks)         & \textbf{9 (30.0\%)} \\
Dormant                                  & 21 (70.0\%) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Commenting out the
\texttt{tm.set\_hybrid\_physics\_mode}\-\texttt{(True)} and
\texttt{tm.set\_hybrid\_physics\_radius}\-\texttt{(70.0)} calls
(commit \texttt{a9435f9}):

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
NPCs spawned                             & 30 \\
Moving ($> 0.1$\,m in 100 ticks)         & \textbf{29 (96.7\%)} \\
Dormant                                  & 1 (3.3\%) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The hybrid-physics setting had been introduced earlier as a
defensive fix for an apparently-frozen-NPC observation in a prior
run, with a paper-cited rationale (CARLA issue \#3860). What we
failed to verify at that time was that the mode \emph{itself}---which
in sync mode puts NPCs outside the ego's physics radius into a
dormant state---was the freezer. Approximately 70\% of every NPC
field across all six training runs, \emph{and the 100k-frame BC
dataset}, was frozen.

that BC captured \emph{some} behavior from the BehaviorAgent expert.
The single best episode (index~16) completed 32.9\% of its route at
7.4\,m/s before a collision. The 75\% collision rate, combined with
the 0\% stuck rate and 5.37\,m/s average speed, is
Codevilla~et~al.'s~\cite{codevilla} distribution-shift signature: BC
trained against frozen NPCs has no learned representation of moving
obstacles' trajectories, so at evaluation time against dynamic NPCs
it drives at expert speed directly into them. The 25\% OFF\_ROUTE
rate is the residual BC imitation error at intersections and sharp
turns.

Ordering: success $>$ crash $>$ creep $>$ sit-still.
Sit-still is strictly dominated; crash is cheaper than creep
(correctly pushing the agent to try real driving over safe
pointless motion); success is the only positive-return option.
This ordering is what the \texttt{idle\_cost}~$= -0.15$ term buys,
and it is the reason run~1's specific failure mode did not recur in
runs~2--6. It is also why the subsequent plateau at 5--6\%~RC
cannot be attributed to the sit-still attractor---once
\texttt{idle\_cost} is in place the agent is economically
incentivized to move.

\section{Lessons Learned}
\label{sec:lessons}

These are the six concrete lessons this project produced.

\textbf{L1. Change one axis at a time, especially near the deadline.}
The most damaging single pattern was layering infrastructure
``improvements'' (TrafficManager caching, inline spectator updates,
\texttt{world.tick(seconds=10)} timeouts) on top of experiment
changes (reward profile, state dimension, $\log\sigma$ clamp) on the
same branch. When a run failed we then had four to six simultaneous
independent variables, and our attribution of cause-to-effect was
statistically equivalent to guessing.

\textbf{L2. A standalone diagnostic is worth ten gradient updates.}
The NPC-motion sanity check that found the root cause took ten
minutes to write and one invocation to falsify the assumption that
hybrid physics was harmless. Five prior runs (15+\,hours of compute)
did not falsify the same assumption because the training loop's
symptoms were mis-attributed at each stage. If the environment is
suspected to be the problem, leave the training loop and ask the
environment directly.

\textbf{L3. PPO metrics do not describe the environment.}
Run~6 produced the healthiest PPO diagnostics of the entire project
(\texttt{approx\_kl}~$= 0.0015$, \texttt{clip\_fraction}~$= 0.08$,
\texttt{explained\_variance}~$= 0.848$) while the actor was frozen
and the NPCs were dormant. An RL agent training against a broken
environment will happily report perfect policy-improvement metrics
while learning nothing about the intended task.

\textbf{L4. Pre-register falsifiers.} Every run in
Sec.~\ref{sec:results} was launched with an explicit ``if we observe
$X$ at time $T$, the hypothesis is dead'' criterion. This is what
prevented us from continuing any single run past its point of
diagnostic information (e.g., stopping v1 at 233\,k steps instead
of 3\,M). Without pre-registration, confirmation bias is very strong
and the deadline pressure rewards confirmation.

\textbf{L5. BC distribution shift is real and silent.}
The 75\% collision rate in the final eval is not a policy-quality
problem, it is a dataset problem. 100k frames of expert behavior
against frozen NPCs does not generalize to moving NPCs no matter
how well BC converges (final NLL~$= -2.93$, MAE~$= 0.05$). Future
work should verify dataset coverage before training, not after
evaluation.

\textbf{L6. AI coding assistants need scientific memory.}
This project was developed in close collaboration with an AI
coding assistant. The assistant's persistent value came from a
single \texttt{PROJECT\_NOTES.md} file updated after every material
decision, containing the paper citation for each design choice, the
pre-registered falsifier for each run, and the post-hoc analysis of
each failure. Without this file, the iteration loop would have
re-derived the same reasoning each session and would likely have
re-introduced deleted failure modes. With it, the assistant
correctly declined to re-introduce non-potential cos-heading shaping
when proposed under time pressure---the memory file remembered that
the 7\,M-step legacy run had failed on exactly that term.

\section{Conclusion}

UrbanZero did not produce a route-completing CARLA PPO agent at the
$\sim$15M-step compute scale available to a solo course project. It
did produce three things we believe have standalone scientific
value: a complete open-source CARLA + PPO + BC pipeline, a
pre-registered catalogue of six failure modes each tied to the
published result that predicted it, and the demonstration that a
single line of environment setup code can silently invalidate
fifteen hours of compute in ways that no standard PPO metric
reveals. The final deliverable---a frozen Behavior Cloning policy
achieving 7.42\% mean route completion with a 32.93\% best-episode
peak---is best understood not as the answer to the original research
question, but as the upper envelope on what a BehaviorAgent-imitating
policy can do when the training distribution is itself contaminated.

Future work falls into three buckets. First, re-collect the BC
dataset against \emph{dynamic} NPCs (trivial, $\sim$1~hour of
compute) and retrain; Codevilla~et~al.~\cite{codevilla} and the
reward-economics of Sec.~5 jointly predict this alone should close
most of the collision gap. Second, fully implement Roach's
\cite{roach} KL-to-BC regularization with $\beta = 0.1 \to 0.05$
over 2M finetune steps---the one intervention this project scoped
out due to implementation burden and that the BC+PPO~v3 failure
(run~6) most directly implicates. Third, scale pure-RL compute by
$50\times$ to match CaRL~\cite{carl} and measure where the plateau
actually is when the environment is not contaminated; the present
project cannot distinguish between ``5\% is the hardware ceiling''
and ``5\% is the NPC-freeze ceiling,'' and that distinction will
matter for any follow-up.
